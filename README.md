The project follows the design, 3D printing and testing phases for a electric robotic gripper. The demonstration phase is emphasizing on the functionality of the gripper through the use of a simple application, the opening and closing of the fingers through a potentiometer.
The main reason for tackling this theme was its relevance in the current state of industrial robotics.

Furthermore this project is also a good way in demonstrating the knowledge, relevant to the industrial field, accumulated during the last four years of university.

Like the overview suggests, there were three main objectives to achieve in order to validate the project itself:
  - The design phase -  where the main mechanism for the robotic gripper was considered. The prototype went through three stages of developement suffering major changes in its overall structure and design in order to satisfy the mechanical and functional needs.
  - The assembly phase - which consists in 3D printing the final prototype components and assembling them. Key considerations where taken during the design phase of the components for the robotic gripper: a general margin of error when dimensioning the parts to compensate for the material shrinkage as it cools down.
  - The demonstration phase - where the functionality of robotic gripper is highlighted in a simple application: using a potentiometer we control the movements of the gripper fingers.

A roadmap was created prior to the development phase, for a better organisation of the entire process, while also serving as a great tool in presenting the progress and the priority of each individual phase.
  - First a starting point was chosen, a source of inspiration for the robotic gripper
  - Next was a series of three prototypes, each one bringing improvements to the overall functionality of the product
  - After that came the 3D printing and assembly of the robotic gripper.
  - Lastly was the demonstration phase where the gripper was connected to a potentiometer through a electric circuit, using an Arduino board.
